Understanding MCAD Deficiency

Medium-Chain Acyl-CoA Dehydrogenase Deficiency

Overview Mutation Biology Treatment Statistics Community Sources

Overview

MCAD Deficiency (Medium-Chain Acyl-Coenzyme A Dehydrogenase Deficiency) is a rare genetic metabolic disorder that impairs the body's ability to break down medium-chain fatty acids. The lack of metabolic breakdown of medium chain fatty acids can lead to hypoglycemia muscle breakdown, liver and brain damage if not treated properly.

Mutation & Inheritance

MCAD deficiency is caused by mutations in the ACADM gene and is an autosomal recessive inheritance trait. This means that a child would have to inherit two copies of the mutated gene, one from each parent to be affected by MCAD deficiency. Children with only one copy of the gene are known as carriers and are not affected by MCAD deficiency.

Biology of MCAD Deficiency

The MCAD enzyme, located within the mitochondria, is responsible for breaking down medium-chain fatty acids into energy. When the enzyme is deficient, these fatty acids can accumulate, leading to metabolic stress, liver dysfunction, brain damage and muscle breakdown. Additionally, MCAD deficiency can lead to energy deficits and can cause lethargy in individuals due to inefficient use of the energy from Medium-Chain-Fatty-Acids.

Treatment & Management

Although there is no cure for MCAD deficiency can be managed through dietary changes. A high-carbohydrate diet and low-fat diet along with the avoidance of prolonged fasting are recommended to maintain stable blood sugar levels. During illnesses or metabolic stress, individuals are often prescribed glucose supplementation to prevent metabolic crises. There is also ongoing research for glycerol phenylbutyrate supplementation for prevention of hypoglycemia in individuals under 16 years old.

Statistics & Facts

MCAD deficiency affects approximately 1 in 17,000 newborns primarily northern European infants. Thanks to routine newborn programs, early diagnosis has become more common and allows children to be treated quickly, reducing the risk of severe metabolic complications. Unfortunately, infants with MCAD deficiency often die of sudden death due to feeding irregularities or other issues.

Community Awareness & Support

People with MCAD deficiency can be supported through education of the public especially since they are at risk of sudden death. Additionally, education can be used to identify if individuals are having a metabolic crisis since metabolic crises can lead to permanent brain damage and the development of several psychological disorders such as Aphasia or permanent muscle weakness.
